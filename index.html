The Studio

Our Story

The Stephens Tapestry Studio has its roots in the north of Swaziland — now Eswatini — a tiny monarchy between South Africa and Mozambique.

In the late 1940s, Coral Stephens moved from Johannesburg with her husband Robert to a remote mountainous region where he established a commercial forestry operation. Her friends had given her a small loom to keep her occupied. Coral had no intention of being bored.

Sylvia Mantanga, a Xhosa woman who came with Coral from Johannesburg, had been taught to weave by Swedish missionaries in the Eastern Cape — where a burgeoning industry of angora goat farming was already taking place. Angora goats produce the rich, robust mohair with which Coral Stephens fabrics became synonymous.

Coral Stephens at Boshimela, Eswatini
1940s

Boshimela

Coral and Robert establish a life in the mountains. News spreads about the curtains and carpets adorning the house. Orders begin to flow — first from houseguests, then from their friends.

1960s

Cecil Skotnes & The First Tapestry

Marguerite Stephens, Coral's eldest daughter, attended an exhibition of South African artist Cecil Skotnes. Coral declared that her daughter would weave one of the woodblock works. It sold instantly — and a career was born. The woodblock still hangs in Marguerite's Johannesburg home.

1980s

The Royal Hotel Commission

A monumental 23-metre tapestry, created in collaboration with Judith Mason, was commissioned for the Royal Hotel in Durban — where it still hangs today.

1990s

William Kentridge

Marguerite met a young William Kentridge. Immediately taken with the amplified textural interpretation of his work, he has since collaborated extensively with the studio — propelling Stephens Tapestry to the world's greatest museums and private collections.

2001

Murrae Stephens

Coral's grand-daughter in law, Murrae Stephens, takes over the business. Under her direction the studio continues to thrive and Coral Stephens handwoven fabrics become a mainstay for interior designers worldwide.

2016

Christine Weavind

The third generation of Stephens women. Christine formally took over the studio at the end of 2016. She first learned to weave at age 7, sitting beside her mother at the loom. The Johannesburg and Eswatini studios combined now employ 30 women — some grandchildren of the original team.

"The Stephens Tapestry Studio is an all women operation — except of course for some of the artists — and some of the goats." — Christine Weavind
Craft

The Process

Each tapestry is the result of months of patient, skilled handwork — a collaboration between artist vision and weaver craft that spans two countries.

01

Carding & Spinning

We begin with mohair from goats in the Eastern Cape. Carding and spinning happens in Eswatini, often as homestead work. This gives the women flexibility — they can spin while looking after children, farming, or managing other responsibilities.

02

Dyeing

Dyeing is a patient and intuitive process. We work with mohair because its natural sheen and lustre take colour with remarkable brilliance — giving our tapestries their signature vibrancy. Over the years the studio has developed several hundred colour charts, each colour assigned a number.

03

Cartoon Development

Once the artist has completed the design, a high-resolution image is printed to guide annotation and weaving. A second print — produced at the exact scale of the tapestry — becomes the "cartoon": the full-sized template placed behind the warp threads, serving as the weaver's visual map for colour, form and detail throughout the weaving.

04

Weaving

Teams of up to six weavers translate and amplify the artist's original design into tapestries that hang in museums and private collections around the world. Depending on the size and intricacy of the work, the weaving process can take between one and six months.

Collaborations

Artists

For more than six decades, Stephens Tapestry Studio has collaborated with some of the world's most celebrated artists — translating works of singular vision into monumental woven form.

Artist

William Kentridge

South Africa's most celebrated contemporary artist. Kentridge's collaboration with the studio began in the 1990s when he met Marguerite Stephens and was immediately captivated by the amplified textural interpretation of his work. His tapestries are exhibited in the world's leading museums and are coveted by collectors globally.

Sold and exhibited through Goodman Gallery (Johannesburg, Cape Town, London), Marian Goodman Gallery (New York, Paris, London) and Lia Rumma Gallery (Naples, Milan).

Series
The Porter Series Triumph & Laments Series The Metro Series The Nose Series Royal Academy Tapestries The Manet Series Various Tapestries
Artist

Sam Nhlengethwa

Renowned for his vibrant, jazz-inflected compositions, Sam Nhlengethwa's works translate into tapestry with a remarkable dynamism. His scenes of township life, music, and urban energy find extraordinary new life in mohair.

Works
After Miles Davis Jazz Night Kippies, Johannesburg Nightshift Drum Underground
Artist

Robert Hodgins

Robert Hodgins's bold, satirical figurative works — populated by his characteristic buffoonish characters — translate into tapestry with unexpected grandeur. His wry humour and vivid palette make for some of the studio's most striking woven pieces.

Works
Roccoco 1 Roccoco 2 Queen of Spades King of Hearts The Emperor Blue Suit Guy
Artist

Diane Victor

Diane Victor's unflinching, psychologically charged imagery — drawn in smoke, ash, and charcoal — finds a haunting counterpart in the tactile richness of woven mohair. Her works with the studio occupy a unique intersection of vulnerability and material permanence.

Works
Bareback (In Studio) Bareback

The studio has also worked with Judith Mason, Nel Erasmus, Graeme Black, Walter Battiss, Penny Siopis, Norman Catherine, Cecil Skotnes, and Misha Kahn, among others.
