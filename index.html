The studio produces both original artworks and bespoke commissions, crafting tapestries that exist in the long tradition of woven art — yet are entirely of the present moment.

About the Medium

What is Tapestry?

Tapestry is a form of textile art, woven on a loom, and composed of two sets of interlaced threads, those running parallel to the length (called the warp) and those running parallel to the width (called the weft). The warp threads are set up on the loom before weaving begins, and the weft threads are woven back and forth across part or all of the warps to create the design.

"Tapestry is one of the oldest and most distinguished art forms in human history — a tradition spanning millennia, practised across cultures and continents."

The structure of a tapestry differs from most woven textiles in that the weft threads do not necessarily run continuously from selvedge to selvedge — instead, the weaver works different coloured threads into specific areas of the warp, building up the image section by section. This is what allows the tapestry weaver to create the complex pictorial designs and tonal gradations characteristic of the medium.

Unlike a printed reproduction or a painting, a tapestry has genuine physical texture and depth. Each thread is individually placed by the weaver's hand, and the resulting surface catches the light in a way that is unique to woven fabric. A tapestry changes as you walk past it, as the light shifts throughout the day, and as the seasons change.

The warp threads in a tapestry are typically hidden in the finished work, covered by the weft. The weft threads carry the colour and pattern, and are beaten down firmly with each row to produce a smooth, even surface. In a high-warp tapestry such as those made at Stephen's Tapestry Studio, the warp runs vertically on the loom, and the weaver works from the back, using a mirror or working from a cartoon placed behind the warp threads to follow the design.

The technique is slow, demanding, and requires considerable skill and experience. A large tapestry may take months or even years to complete. This investment of time and skill is part of what gives tapestry its weight and presence as an art form — each piece is genuinely unique, the result of thousands of individual decisions made by the weaver in the course of creating the work.

STS — 001
Koi

An original tapestry depicting koi fish in a pond. The koi tapestry is a beautifully detailed and vibrant work, depicting a group of koi fish moving through water. The fish are shown in a variety of colours — orange, white, black and gold — and the whole composition has a strong sense of movement and life.

The water is rendered in rippling tones of blue and green, with dappled light playing across the surface. The piece is typical of Stephen's work in its combination of close observation and lively handling of colour and texture.

Wool & silk · Hand-woven on upright loom
STS — 002
Sunflowers

A tapestry depicting a vase of sunflowers. The Sunflowers tapestry is a richly coloured work, showing a large bunch of sunflowers in a vase. The flowers are full and open, with deep golden yellow petals surrounding dark centres, and the whole composition radiates warmth and energy.

The background is a warm, deep tone that allows the flowers to stand out with full force. The piece shows Stephen's characteristic ability to handle complex shapes and textures within the tapestry medium.

Wool & silk · Hand-woven on upright loom
STS — 003
Abstract Study

An abstract tapestry exploring form, colour, and the physical properties of the woven medium. Abstract works allow the weaver to focus on the intrinsic qualities of the tapestry — the way threads interlock, the texture of the surface, the subtle blending of colour that occurs when adjacent threads interact in the woven structure.

This piece demonstrates the expressive potential of tapestry as a purely abstract medium, with forms and colours that emerge from the weaving process itself.

Wool · Hand-woven on upright loom
STS — 004
Landscape

A tapestry depicting a landscape scene. Landscape has been one of the great subjects of tapestry weaving throughout the history of the medium. In woven form, landscape takes on a particular quality — the horizontal lines of the weft giving the image a natural rhythm that suits the structure of sky, land and water.

Stephen's landscape tapestries draw on the long tradition of picturing the natural world in woven form, while bringing a contemporary sensitivity to colour and composition.

The Studio Practice

How a tapestry
is made

01
Design & Cartoon
The process begins with the development of a design — a cartoon — which serves as the template for the woven image. This may be an original composition or based on a provided image or brief.
02
Setting the Warp
The loom is warped up — threads are strung vertically at a consistent tension across the width of the frame. The cartoon is placed behind the warp as a reference for the weaver.
03
Weaving
Working from the bottom up, the weaver interlaces coloured weft threads into the warp, building up the image row by row. The weft is beaten down firmly with each pass to produce an even, smooth surface.
04
Finishing
On completion, the tapestry is cut from the loom and the loose warp ends are secured. The finished piece is mounted for display, typically on a wooden batten or stretcher.
The Artist

Stephen
Blackmore

Portrait

Stephen Blackmore has been weaving tapestries for many years. He trained in the traditional techniques of tapestry weaving and has developed a practice that encompasses both original works and commissioned pieces.

Stephen works on a traditional high-warp loom, using the same fundamental techniques that tapestry weavers have used for centuries. His subjects range from figurative works — portraits, still lifes, landscapes — to more abstract explorations of colour and form.

His work is characterised by close attention to colour — the way colours interact and modify one another within the woven structure — and by a sensitivity to the particular qualities of light and texture that make tapestry such a distinctive medium.

Stephen takes commissions for original tapestry works, working closely with clients to develop pieces that meet their specific requirements and work well in their intended setting. He is happy to discuss potential projects and can provide advice on scale, subject, colour palette, and other aspects of the commission process.

Artist's Statement
"I am drawn to tapestry because it is slow. In an age of instant images and digital reproduction, there is something valuable about a medium that demands patience and sustained attention — from the maker and from the viewer. Each tapestry is the result of many hours of careful, concentrated work, and I hope that this quality of attention is felt in the finished piece."
Bespoke Work

Commissions

Working with Stephen

Stephen welcomes commissions for original tapestry works. He is happy to work with individual clients, interior designers, architects, and organisations to develop tapestries for specific spaces and purposes.

Commissions may be based on a supplied image or design, or developed collaboratively with the client. Stephen is experienced in working from photographs, paintings, illustrations, and other reference material, as well as developing original designs from scratch.

Begin a Conversation

The Commission Process

The first step is an initial conversation — to discuss the intended setting, the subject or type of design, the required size, and any other relevant considerations. Stephen will then provide a quotation for the work, along with an indication of the likely timescale.

On agreement of terms, a deposit is required before work commences. Stephen will provide regular updates during the weaving process, and the client is welcome to visit the studio to see work in progress. The balance is payable on completion, prior to delivery or collection.

Tapestries may be shipped anywhere in the world. Stephen can advise on the best methods of packing and shipping for tapestry works.
